# Governance Gap Analyzer

The Governance Gap Analyzer is a **free, self-serve assessment tool** that evaluates an organization's governance maturity across five critical dimensions. It serves as the primary **lead qualification mechanism** for the AINEFF product suite — identifying organizations with governance gaps and routing them to the appropriate paid engagement.

## Product Overview

| Attribute | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| **Category** | Lead Generation / Assessment Tool |
| **Price Point** | Free (lead generation) |
| **Target Market** | CEOs, CTOs, COOs, Compliance Officers at mid-market companies |
| **Core Value** | Quantify governance risk exposure in under 5 minutes |
| **Delivery Model** | Self-serve web tool with instant results |
| **Strategic Role** | Qualification filter → pipeline generator → upsell trigger |
| **Phase Activation** | Phase 0 |
| **Confidence Score** | 90% |

## Assessment Framework

The analyzer evaluates five dimensions of governance maturity, each scored 1-5:

| Dimension | Question Focus | Weight | Risk Domain |
|-----------|---------------|--------|-------------|
| **AI Governance Purpose** | Does your organization have a defined purpose for AI governance? | 25% | Strategic |
| **Human Liability Mapping** | Are human decision-makers mapped to AI-influenced outcomes? | 25% | Legal/Liability |
| **Chokepoint Identification** | Are workflow bottlenecks identified and costed? | 20% | Operational |
| **Pre-Incident Governance** | Do you review decisions for survivability BEFORE execution? | 20% | Risk |
| **ORF Protocol Alignment** | Does your governance framework follow constitutional coordination principles? | 10% | Structural |

## The 5 Questions

### Question 1: AI Governance Purpose

> **"Does your organization have a formally documented purpose for how AI governance supports business objectives?"**

| Score | Response | Interpretation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | No — we have not considered AI governance | Critical gap — no governance awareness |
| 2 | Informally — some people think about it, nothing documented | Awareness exists but no structure |
| 3 | Partially — we have some policies but they are not comprehensive | Foundation exists, needs formalization |
| 4 | Mostly — documented governance purpose, partially implemented | Strong foundation, needs operational integration |
| 5 | Yes — fully documented, implemented, and regularly reviewed | Mature governance — optimize and expand |

### Question 2: Human Liability Mapping

> **"For every AI-influenced decision in your organization, can you identify the specific human who is accountable if that decision causes harm?"**

| Score | Response | Interpretation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | No — we have no idea who is responsible for AI decisions | Severe liability exposure |
| 2 | Vaguely — "the team" or "the department" is responsible | Diffused accountability = no accountability |
| 3 | Partially — some decisions have named owners, most do not | Inconsistent protection |
| 4 | Mostly — named owners for critical decisions, gaps in routine ones | Good coverage, needs completeness |
| 5 | Yes — every AI-influenced decision has a named accountable human | Full liability mapping in place |

### Question 3: Chokepoint Identification

> **"Have you identified and quantified the cost of workflow bottlenecks (chokepoints) in your core business processes?"**

| Score | Response | Interpretation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | No — we do not track workflow bottlenecks | Invisible waste — likely significant |
| 2 | Anecdotally — people complain but we have not measured | Known pain, no data |
| 3 | Partially — some processes mapped, costs not quantified | Awareness without action |
| 4 | Mostly — major chokepoints identified and costed | Strong operational awareness |
| 5 | Yes — all chokepoints mapped, costed, and actively managed | Operational maturity — ready for optimization |

### Question 4: Pre-Incident Governance

> **"Before approving a significant decision, do you formally evaluate whether that decision would survive investigation if something goes wrong?"**

| Score | Response | Interpretation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | No — we make decisions and hope for the best | Maximum pre-incident exposure |
| 2 | Informally — gut feel and experience guide decisions | Subjective risk management |
| 3 | Sometimes — for big decisions, we do some analysis | Inconsistent protection |
| 4 | Usually — formal review for major decisions, informal for others | Good practice, needs universality |
| 5 | Always — every significant decision passes a survivability review | PIAR-equivalent in place |

### Question 5: ORF Protocol Alignment

> **"Does your governance framework ensure that coordination authority and revenue generation are structurally separated?"**

| Score | Response | Interpretation |
|-------|----------|---------------|
| 1 | I do not understand the question | No constitutional governance awareness |
| 2 | No — the same people who set rules also profit from them | Structural corruption risk |
| 3 | Partially — some separation exists but is not enforced | Weak structural protection |
| 4 | Mostly — separation by policy, not by architecture | Policy-based (breakable) protection |
| 5 | Yes — architectural separation between coordination and revenue | Constitutional governance in place |

|------------------------|---------------|--------------|------------|
| **5-8** | Critical | Extreme — immediate action required | Red |

| **13-16** | Developing | Moderate — foundation exists, gaps remain | Yellow |
