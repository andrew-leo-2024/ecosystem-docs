# Learning Domain Taxonomy

The AINEFF Ecosystem's Skills Ontology does not invent knowledge categories from scratch. It maps them from the **complete space of human learning** — every domain in which a human can develop competence, expertise, or mastery. This taxonomy is that map.

It feeds directly into the AINEOUTMJS (AI-Native Enterprise Organization Unit Team Member Job Skills) layer — the atomic bottom of the entity hierarchy. Every skill assigned to every team member in every enterprise traces back to a learning domain cataloged here.

---

## Why Completeness Matters

Most organizations manage skills with ad-hoc lists: "Python," "leadership," "project management." This produces blind spots — entire domains of human capability that are invisible to the talent system.

The AINEFF approach is different: **start with the complete space of human knowledge, then derive skills from it.** If a domain exists in human learning, the Skills Ontology must be able to represent it. Anything less creates structural ignorance.

## The Complete Taxonomy

### 1. Languages

The foundational domain of human communication and symbolic reasoning.

- **Natural Languages** — Acquisition, fluency, and mastery of spoken and written languages (English, Mandarin, Arabic, Spanish, Hindi, etc.)
- **Classical Languages** — Latin, Ancient Greek, Sanskrit, Classical Arabic, Classical Chinese
- **Sign Languages** — ASL, BSL, and regional sign language systems
- **Constructed Languages** — Esperanto, formal logic languages, programming-adjacent communication languages
- **Linguistics** — Phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics, sociolinguistics, psycholinguistics, computational linguistics
- **Translation & Interpretation** — Cross-lingual knowledge transfer, simultaneous interpretation, literary translation, technical translation, localization

## Maintenance Protocol

This taxonomy is a **living document**. New fields emerge (quantum computing was not a learning domain 30 years ago), fields merge (bioinformatics emerged from biology + computer science), and fields sunset. The taxonomy must be reviewed annually and updated when:

1. A new field achieves institutional recognition (university departments, professional bodies)
2. An existing field splits into distinct sub-domains with separate career paths
3. An existing field is absorbed into a broader domain
4. The Skills Ontology encounters a skill that cannot be traced to any domain

**Completeness is a design requirement, not an aspiration.**
