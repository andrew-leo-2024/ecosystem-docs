# Management Model Catalog (Enterprise Choice)

The AINEFF platform offers a catalog of organizational formalizations that enterprises choose from during onboarding. These are not culture choices -- they are **information-routing architectures** that formalize how decisions move, how work decomposes, and how risk propagates.

Each model maps to a different agent topology configuration. The platform configures agent topology, reporting cadence, escalation paths, and dashboard views accordingly.

---

## Overview: Initial Topology Models

| Model | Agent Organization | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Spotify Model | Squads (Jarvis + agents), Tribes (verticals), Chapters (specializations), Guilds (shared knowledge) | Autonomous, fast-moving product teams |
| SAFe (Scaled Agile) | ARTs (Agile Release Trains), PIs (Program Increments), teams organized in value streams | Large enterprise, regulated industries |
| Scrum@Scale | Scrum of Scrums, Meta-Scrum for coordination, Executive Action Team | Companies already using Scrum |
| LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum) | Feature teams, one Product Owner per product, minimal process | Simpler scaling, co-located teams |
| Team Topologies | Stream-aligned, Platform, Enabling, Complicated-subsystem teams | Clear team interaction patterns |
| DORA/DevOps | Deployment frequency, lead time, MTTR, change failure rate | Engineering-performance-focused orgs |
| Kanban Enterprise | WIP limits, flow metrics, pull-based work distribution | Continuous delivery, ops-heavy work |

### Agent Topology Implications

- **Spotify** -- autonomous squads with loose coupling
- **SAFe** -- coordinated trains with PI planning cadence
- **Scrum@Scale** -- hierarchical Scrum Masters (Jarvis hierarchy)
- **Team Topologies** -- typed agent teams with explicit interaction modes

## Catalog Models 1-10: Execution Topology

### 1. Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe)

Industrial synchronization. Agile Release Trains. Program Increments. Heavy portfolio layer.

**What it actually does:** Synchronizes large numbers of teams, creates predictable planning cycles, introduces ceremony as control mechanism.

**Risk profile:** Bureaucratic bloat. Process worship. PI theater. But it makes chaos legible to executives.

**Agent topology implication:** Train-based orchestration layer. Central PI planning agents. Strong reporting cadence. Escalation lattice baked in.

**Use when:** You need traceability, regulatory defensibility, and synchronized releases across dozens of teams.

### 2. Spotify Model

Optimizes for speed and autonomy. Squads own problems end-to-end. Tribes loosely coordinate. Chapters maintain craft coherence. Guilds spread knowledge horizontally.

**What it actually does:** Pushes decision authority downward, accepts redundancy, tolerates local optimization. Anti-centralization by design.

**Risk profile:** Coordination drift. Duplication of effort. Shadow standards. In regulated industries, this becomes compliance roulette.

**Agent topology implication:** Independent "Jarvis cells" with weak inter-squad coupling.

**Use when:** Product velocity \> regulatory coherence.

### 3. Scrum@Scale

Hierarchy of Scrum Masters. Meta-Scrum for product alignment. Fractal Scrum.

**What it actually does:** Replicates Scrum vertically, keeps coordination lightweight, preserves local autonomy.

**Risk profile:** Coordination gaps between layers. Authority ambiguity. Depends heavily on leadership maturity.

**Agent topology implication:** Recursive Jarvis layers. Each Scrum-of-Scrums node aggregates and reconciles signals upward.

**Use when:** You want to scale Scrum without installing a full industrial governance stack.

### 4. Large-Scale Scrum (LeSS)

Minimalism taken seriously. One Product Owner. Feature teams. Few artifacts.

**What it actually does:** Reduces coordination complexity, forces clarity of product ownership, avoids layered bureaucracy.

**Risk profile:** Breaks under geographic dispersion. Fails if product boundaries are muddy.

**Agent topology implication:** Flat topology. High transparency. Minimal meta-layer.

**Use when:** Single product, high cohesion, co-located or tightly integrated teams.

### 5. Team Topologies

A structural taxonomy, not a scaling framework. Stream-aligned teams. Platform teams. Enabling teams. Complicated subsystem teams. Explicit interaction modes.

**What it actually does:** Makes team boundaries explicit, controls cognitive load, optimizes communication pathways.

**Risk profile:** Misclassification leads to structural bottlenecks. Requires architectural discipline.

**Agent topology implication:** Typed agent classes. Interaction contracts. Controlled interfaces. Maps extremely well to AI-governed enterprise systems.

**Use when:** You care about long-term system maintainability and platform thinking.

### 6. DevOps Research and Assessment (DORA Model)

Not a structural model. A performance instrumentation model. Deployment frequency. Lead time. MTTR. Change failure rate.

**What it actually does:** Turns engineering into measurable flow physics.

**Risk profile:** Gaming metrics. Optimizing for speed over safety. Ignoring non-software value streams.

**Agent topology implication:** Telemetry-first architecture. Agents instrumented at every workflow transition.

**Use when:** You need empirical performance feedback loops.

### 7. Kanban Enterprise

Flow over iteration. WIP limits. Pull systems.

**What it actually does:** Reveals bottlenecks mercilessly.

**Risk profile:** Local optimization. Strategy drift if not tied to value streams.

**Agent topology implication:** Queue-aware agents. Flow-state visibility. Bottleneck detection modules.

**Use when:** Operational work dominates. Incident response. Infrastructure. Legal review pipelines.

### 8. Holacracy

Role-based governance. Distributed authority. Structured tension processing.

**What it formalizes:** Distributed authority with explicit rulebooks.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Executive bottlenecks.

**Risk:** Decision latency under scale. Requires cultural maturity.

**Best for:** Companies seeking radical decentralization with explicit rulebooks.

### 9. McKinsey Three Horizons Model

Core (H1), Adjacent (H2), Transformational (H3) portfolio separation.

**What it formalizes:** Temporal separation of exploitation vs exploration.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Innovation cannibalizing the cash engine.

**Risk:** H3 theater without real funding. Core bias suffocates adjacencies.

**Best for:** Large enterprises trying to innovate without destabilizing revenue.

### 10. Toyota Lean Operating System

Continuous improvement (Kaizen). Waste elimination. Gemba-based learning.

**What it formalizes:** Operational excellence through waste elimination and feedback loops.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Hidden inefficiency and defect propagation.

**Risk:** Cargo-cult lean without cultural discipline.

**Best for:** Industrial, supply chain, and operational excellence contexts.

## Catalog Models 11-20: Governance and Performance Control

### 11. Objectives and Key Results (OKR)

Quarterly objective cascades. Measurable key results. Public scoring.

**What it formalizes:** Strategic alignment through measurable outcome contracts.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Strategic drift.

**Risk:** Metric theater. Sandbagging. Output over outcome confusion.

### 12. Balanced Scorecard

Four perspectives: financial, customer, internal process, learning & growth.

**What it formalizes:** Multi-dimensional performance visibility.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Financial-only blindness.

### 13. Management by Objectives (MBO)

Top-down objective setting with individual accountability.

**What it formalizes:** Individual performance contracts.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Ambiguity of responsibility.

### 14. Sociocracy

Consent-based decision-making in semi-autonomous circles.

**What it formalizes:** Distributed authority with structured feedback loops.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Executive bottlenecks.

### 15. RACI Governance Model

Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed matrices.

**What it formalizes:** Decision role clarity.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Decision ambiguity and shadow ownership.

### 16. PMO Model

Centralized Project Management Office. Stage gates. Portfolio oversight.

**What it formalizes:** Capital allocation discipline across initiatives.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Runaway projects and resource sprawl.

### 17. Business Process Reengineering (BPR)

Radical redesign of end-to-end processes.

**What it formalizes:** Process-centric optimization over departmental silos.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Legacy inefficiency inertia.

### 18. ITIL Service Model

Service lifecycle governance. Incident, problem, change management controls.

**What it formalizes:** Operational reliability.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Uncontrolled change and service instability.

### 19. Scaled Agile Lean Portfolio Management (LPM)

Strategy-to-epic funding alignment. Guardrails for decentralized spending.

**What it formalizes:** Strategic capital flow control.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Portfolio chaos.

### 20. Beyond Budgeting Model

Dynamic forecasting. Decentralized financial authority. Rolling planning.

**What it formalizes:** Adaptive capital governance.

**Failure mode it optimizes against:** Annual budget rigidity.

## Strategic Architecture: The Design Space

Enterprises are not choosing "how to work." They are choosing:

- **Where authority concentrates**
- **How capital flows**
- **How risk is measured**
- **How decisions escalate**
- **How failure is absorbed**

The catalog spans three macro-layers:

1. **Internal execution topology** -- how work flows
2. **Governance and capital allocation logic** -- how power, incentives, and capital flow
3. **Market and ecosystem architecture** -- how the enterprise interfaces with markets

### Composable Control Primitives

The platform abstracts these as topology templates:

- **Node types:** squad, train, stream, platform
- **Edge contracts:** collaborate, serve, enable, escalate
- **Cadence configuration:** iteration, PI, continuous flow
- **Governance intensity:** lightweight, regulated, defense-grade

### Core Dimensional Axes

Every model is a point in a multidimensional space defined by:

| Dimension | Range |
|---|---|
| Authority concentration | Centralized \<-\> Distributed |
| Capital patience | Short-term \<-\> Generational |
| Risk tolerance | Conservative \<-\> Aggressive |
| Information permeability | Compartmented \<-\> Transparent |
| Innovation velocity | Incremental \<-\> Breakthrough |
| Ecosystem control | Participation \<-\> Dominance |
| Ownership structure | Concentrated \<-\> Distributed |
| Decision latency | Fast \<-\> Deliberate |

### From Catalog to Configuration Engine

Once modeled parametrically, enterprises stop choosing brands -- they **configure geometry**. The catalog becomes:

- Not a menu of frameworks
- But a **configuration engine** for organizational operating systems
- Capable of diagnosing incompatibilities during onboarding
- Capable of morphing topology while preserving traceability and historical decision lineage

The deeper play: eventually the platform stops exposing model names entirely. It exposes only desired outcomes -- "high autonomy," "regulated," "innovation lab," "cost discipline" -- and the system composes the topology dynamically.

That is when the catalog becomes an **inference engine** -- turning management theory into programmable infrastructure.
